[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: cost of goods sold

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the 2021 reporting periods, cost of goods sold (COGS) showed consistent increases primarily driven by higher volumes and values in used vehicle trade-ins, non-warranty maintenance services, and retail merchandise sales. Management highlighted ongoing cost reduction efforts and localized procurement to maintain vehicle affordability, particularly in China. Sales-type leasing revenue and associated costs became more significant as the leasing offering scaled up during the year. Warranty expenses remained a consistent component of cost of revenues throughout the periods. Despite these cost increases, management noted a reduction in cost of goods sold per vehicle year-over-year in Q2 2021, indicating some operational efficiencies.

Forecasting credibility: None
No forward guidance on cost of goods sold was provided by management in any period, and thus no guidance-to-actual pairs exist to assess forecasting credibility. Without explicit forecasts and subsequent results, it is not possible to judge management's accuracy or reliability in forecasting this topic. The assessment is therefore limited to reported actuals and qualitative commentary.

Trends:
  - Increasing cost of automotive leasing revenue
  - Rising cost of services and other revenue
  - Growth in used vehicle cost of revenue
  - Ongoing cost reduction efforts
  - Increasing sales-type leasing cost of revenue

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Warranty expense as a component of cost of revenues
  - Impact of trade-in volumes and values on used vehicle costs
  - Non-warranty maintenance service cost increases
  - Localized procurement and manufacturing to reduce costs

Key concerns:
  - No forward guidance on cost of goods sold limits ability to evaluate management's forecasting reliability for this line item.
  - Cost increases driven by volume and value growth in used vehicles and services may pressure margins if not offset by cost reductions.
  - Accounting treatment of sales-type leasing revenue and associated costs became more material during 2021, requiring careful tracking.

Conclusion:
Cost of goods sold increased substantially throughout 2021, driven by higher volumes and values in used vehicle trade-ins, increased non-warranty maintenance services, and retail merchandise sales. Management emphasized cost reduction initiatives and localized procurement to maintain vehicle affordability, which contributed to a reported decrease in cost of goods sold per vehicle in Q2 2021. Warranty expenses consistently formed part of cost of revenues. However, the absence of forward guidance on cost of goods sold prevents an assessment of management's forecasting accuracy for this item. Overall, while costs rose with business growth, management's operational efforts appear focused on mitigating cost pressures.
