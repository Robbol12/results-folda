[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: gross margin

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the course of 2021, gross margin trends showed a clear improvement in the automotive segment, with automotive gross margins rising steadily from around 26% in Q1 to over 29% by year-end. Management expressed optimism about gross margin strength despite headwinds, particularly in energy generation and storage, which continued to experience negative margins due to product ramp challenges and manufacturing underutilization. The automotive & services segment also saw margin improvements, driven by cost reductions, factory utilization, and product mix changes. Energy generation and storage margins remained a drag, with negative gross margins persisting throughout the year, although some improvement was noted compared to prior periods.

Forecasting credibility: High
Management's guidance on gross margin strength was generally optimistic and aligned with actual results, particularly in the automotive segment where reported margins consistently met or exceeded expectations. The single guidance/actual pair available for Q4 2021 was met precisely, reinforcing credibility. The detailed filing data supports management's commentary, showing consistent margin improvement in automotive and automotive & services segments. The negative margins in energy generation and storage were transparently disclosed and explained, further supporting the reliability of management's forecasts.

Trends:
  - Automotive gross margin steadily increasing
  - Automotive & services margin improvement
  - Energy generation and storage gross margin negative but improving
  - Cost reduction and factory utilization driving margin gains

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Energy segment margin headwinds due to product ramp and manufacturing underutilization
  - Impact of product mix on overall gross margin
  - Dependence on Shanghai factory exports for margin improvement
  - Potential upside from software and hardware mix changes

Key concerns:
  - Persistent negative gross margins in energy generation and storage segment despite some improvement
  - Temporary manufacturing underutilization affecting Solar Roof margins
  - Phasing out of Powerpack impacting fixed cost absorption and margins

Conclusion:
Gross margin performance in 2021 was characterized by strong and consistent improvement in the automotive segment, with automotive gross margins rising from approximately 26% in Q1 to 29.3% for the full year, driven by cost reductions, increased production efficiency, and favorable product mix. The automotive & services segment also saw margin gains, increasing from 22.4% to 26.9% year-over-year. Conversely, the energy generation and storage segment continued to face challenges, with gross margins declining from 0.9% to -4.6% due to higher Solar Roof proportion, manufacturing underutilization, and product transitions. Management's optimistic guidance on gross margin strength was substantiated by actual results, particularly in automotive, indicating credible forecasting. Overall, gross margin trends reflect operational improvements in core automotive business offset by ongoing challenges in the energy segment.

[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: cost of goods sold

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the 2021 reporting periods, cost of goods sold (COGS) components such as automotive leasing revenue costs, services and other revenue costs, and warranty expenses consistently increased in absolute terms, reflecting higher sales volumes and expanded service activities. Management highlighted ongoing cost reduction efforts and localized procurement to maintain vehicle affordability, particularly in China. Despite these efforts, cost increases were driven by higher used vehicle costs, trade-in volumes, and non-warranty maintenance services. Management occasionally referenced per-vehicle COGS improvements, such as a $5,000 year-over-year reduction in Q2 2021, but no formal forward guidance on COGS was provided. Warranty expenses remained a consistent component of cost of revenues throughout the year.

Forecasting credibility: None
No guidance versus actual pairs for cost of goods sold were provided in the available evidence, preventing any assessment of management's forecasting credibility on this topic. Management did not offer explicit forward guidance on COGS, which is common for accounting line items like this. Therefore, no credibility rating can be assigned based on the data.

Trends:
  - Increasing absolute cost of automotive leasing revenue
  - Rising cost of services and other revenue
  - Growth in warranty expense as part of cost of revenues
  - Ongoing cost reduction efforts and localized procurement
  - Higher used vehicle cost of revenue driven by volume and trade-ins

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Impact of increased sales volumes on COGS
  - Cost pressures from used vehicle trade-ins and retail merchandise
  - Warranty expense as a consistent cost component
  - Localized manufacturing and procurement to control costs

Key concerns:
  - No forward guidance on cost of goods sold limits ability to evaluate management's forecasting accuracy.
  - Cost increases driven by volume and service expansion may pressure margins despite cost reduction efforts.
  - No accounting or reporting inconsistencies were noted across filings regarding COGS.

Conclusion:
Cost of goods sold increased significantly throughout 2021, driven primarily by higher volumes in automotive leasing, used vehicle sales, and service-related activities. Management emphasized cost reduction initiatives and localized procurement to maintain vehicle affordability, which contributed to a reported per-vehicle cost reduction in Q2 2021. Warranty expenses consistently formed part of the cost of revenues. However, the absence of forward guidance on COGS means that management's ability to forecast this line item cannot be assessed. Overall, while costs rose with business growth, management's efforts to control costs appear to have had some positive impact on per-unit costs.
