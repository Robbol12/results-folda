[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: net income

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the reported periods in 2021, Tesla's net income showed a strong upward trajectory, with record production and deliveries contributing to significant increases in GAAP net income. The company surpassed $1 billion in quarterly net income for the first time in Q1 and Q2, and by year-end, the annual net income reached $5.52 billion, marking a substantial improvement over the prior year. The increases were primarily driven by higher net income excluding non-cash expenses and gains, alongside favorable changes in net operating assets and liabilities. Noncontrolling interests showed some variability but generally decreased over the periods. Management highlighted the milestone of accumulated profitability becoming positive by the end of 2021.

Forecasting credibility: None
No forward guidance on net income was provided by management in any of the periods, so there are no guidance versus actual pairs to evaluate forecasting credibility. Therefore, it is not possible to assess the accuracy or reliability of management's net income forecasts based on the available data.

Trends:
  - Strong net income growth throughout 2021
  - Record production and deliveries supporting profitability
  - Increasing net income excluding non-cash expenses and gains
  - Decreasing net income attributable to noncontrolling interests
  - Accumulated profitability turning positive by year-end

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Impact of non-cash expenses and gains on net income
  - Fluctuations due to currency re-measurement and settlement
  - Variability in net operating assets and liabilities

Key concerns:
  - No forward-looking net income guidance limits ability to assess forecasting reliability
  - Currency fluctuations and accounting for non-cash items introduce variability in net income
  - Noncontrolling interests show inconsistent trends, which may affect net income attribution

Conclusion:
Tesla's net income in 2021 demonstrated robust growth, with quarterly and annual figures reflecting significant profitability improvements driven by operational performance and favorable accounting factors. The company achieved a milestone of positive accumulated profitability, underscoring its financial maturation. However, the absence of forward guidance on net income means that management's forecasting credibility on this metric cannot be evaluated. Despite this, the consistent upward trend in reported net income and detailed disclosures in filings provide a transparent view of Tesla's improving financial health.

TOPIC: selling, general and administrative
======================================================================

[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: selling, general and administrative

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the reported periods in 2021, selling, general and administrative (SG&A) expenses showed significant increases, notably a 68% rise in Q1 2021 compared to the prior year. Stock-based compensation, particularly related to the 2018 CEO Performance Award, consistently contributed a substantial non-cash portion of SG&A expenses, increasing from $113 million in Q1 2020 to $386 million in Q1 2021. Cash flow statements repeatedly highlighted that cash investments in SG&A were a key factor affecting operating cash flows, alongside research and development and working capital. Additionally, a $43 million payment related to the SolarCity acquisition was recognized as a reduction in SG&A expenses in mid-2020 and referenced in subsequent filings. Marketing and advertising costs were consistently expensed as incurred within SG&A. No forward guidance on SG&A was provided by management during these periods.

Forecasting credibility: None
There are zero guidance versus actual pairs available for selling, general and administrative expenses, indicating that management does not provide forward guidance on this line item. As a result, it is not possible to assess forecasting credibility for SG&A based on the available data. Any commentary or trends observed are descriptive and do not reflect forecasting accuracy.

Trends:
  - Significant increase in SG&A expenses year-over-year
  - Rising stock-based compensation within SG&A
  - Consistent cash investments impacting operating cash flows
  - Recognition of SolarCity acquisition-related payment reducing SG&A
  - Marketing and advertising costs expensed as incurred

Recurring themes/risks:
  - High stock-based compensation expense volatility
  - Cash flow sensitivity to SG&A spending
  - Non-cash expenses materially affecting SG&A
  - Acquisition-related adjustments impacting SG&A

Key concerns:
  - No forward guidance on SG&A limits ability to evaluate management's forecasting reliability for this expense category.
  - Stock-based compensation is a large and growing component of SG&A, which may obscure underlying operational expense trends.
  - The $43 million SolarCity-related payment recognized as a reduction to SG&A expenses introduces a one-time adjustment that complicates period-to-period comparability.

Conclusion:
Selling, general and administrative expenses increased substantially in 2021, driven in large part by stock-based compensation expenses related to executive awards. These non-cash expenses are a significant and growing portion of SG&A, which management records consistently across periods. Cash flow disclosures emphasize that SG&A spending is a key use of operating cash, alongside R&D and working capital. The recognition of a $43 million payment related to the SolarCity acquisition as a reduction to SG&A expenses is a notable one-time adjustment. Management does not provide forward guidance on SG&A, so forecasting credibility cannot be assessed. Overall, SG&A expenses reflect both operational growth and significant non-cash components, with no direct forecasting transparency from management.
