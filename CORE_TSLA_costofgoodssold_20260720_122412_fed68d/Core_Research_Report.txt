[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: cost of goods sold

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the reported periods in 2021, cost of goods sold (COGS) showed consistent increases primarily driven by higher volumes and values in used vehicle trade-ins, increased non-warranty maintenance services, and retail merchandise sales. Management highlighted ongoing cost reduction efforts and localized procurement to maintain vehicle affordability, particularly in China. Warranty expenses were consistently recorded as part of cost of revenues. While management provided some per-vehicle cost figures and qualitative commentary on cost trends, no explicit forward guidance on COGS was given.

Forecasting credibility: None
There were zero guidance versus actual pairs found for cost of goods sold, indicating management does not provide explicit forward guidance on this line item. Therefore, it is not possible to assess forecasting credibility for COGS based on the available data. Any qualitative commentary or cost trends mentioned cannot substitute for a formal guidance-actual comparison.

Trends:
  - Increasing cost of automotive leasing revenue
  - Rising cost of services and other revenue
  - Higher used vehicle cost of revenue
  - Ongoing cost reduction efforts
  - Localized procurement and manufacturing

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Warranty expense as part of cost of revenues
  - Impact of increased sales volume on costs
  - Non-warranty maintenance service costs
  - Trade-in volume and value fluctuations

Key concerns:
  - No forward guidance on cost of goods sold limits ability to evaluate management's forecasting accuracy for this key expense line.
  - Consistent increases in cost of services and other revenue may pressure margins if not offset by cost reductions or pricing.
  - No accounting or reporting inconsistencies noted; warranty expense treatment remained consistent.

Conclusion:
Cost of goods sold increased notably throughout 2021, driven by higher volumes and values in used vehicle trade-ins, increased non-warranty maintenance services, and retail merchandise sales. Management emphasized cost reduction initiatives and localized procurement to maintain vehicle affordability, which may help mitigate cost pressures. Warranty expenses were consistently included in cost of revenues. However, management did not provide explicit forward guidance on COGS, preventing an assessment of forecasting credibility. The steady rise in COGS components underscores the importance of ongoing cost control to sustain profitability.
