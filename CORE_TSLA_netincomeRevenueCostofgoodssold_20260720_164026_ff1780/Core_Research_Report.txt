[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: net income

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the course of 2021, Tesla's net income showed a strong upward trajectory, with record production and deliveries contributing to significant increases in GAAP net income each quarter. The company surpassed $1 billion in net income for the first time in Q1 and Q2, and by year-end reported $5.52 billion in net income attributable to common stockholders. The filings consistently attribute these gains to improvements in net income excluding non-cash expenses and gains, as well as favorable changes in net operating assets and liabilities. Noncontrolling interests showed some variability but were a smaller component of the overall net income picture. Management highlighted the milestone of accumulated profitability becoming positive by the end of 2021.

Forecasting credibility: None
There were zero guidance versus actual pairs provided for net income, as management did not give explicit forward guidance on this metric. Therefore, it is not possible to assess forecasting credibility for net income based on the available evidence. Any commentary on net income was retrospective or celebratory rather than predictive.

Trends:
  - Strong sequential growth in net income
  - Record production and deliveries driving profitability
  - Increasing net income excluding non-cash items
  - Fluctuations in noncontrolling interests
  - Positive accumulated profitability by year-end

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Impact of non-cash expenses and gains on net income
  - Variability in net operating assets and liabilities
  - Currency fluctuations affecting net income
  - Noncontrolling interests volatility

Key concerns:
  - No forward guidance on net income limits ability to judge forecasting accuracy or management credibility on this metric.
  - Currency re-measurement gains/losses and non-cash items introduce variability that may complicate net income comparability across periods.

Conclusion:
Tesla's net income grew substantially throughout 2021, reaching $5.52 billion for the full year, driven by operational improvements and favorable changes in working capital. Despite the lack of forward guidance, the filings provide a clear and consistent picture of increasing profitability. The company achieved important milestones in profitability, reflecting strong business execution. However, the absence of guidance means forecasting credibility cannot be assessed, and fluctuations from currency effects and non-cash items should be monitored as potential sources of volatility.

[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: Cost of goods sold

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the 2021 reporting periods, cost of goods sold (COGS) showed consistent increases primarily driven by growth in automotive leasing revenue, used vehicle cost of revenue, and costs supporting non-warranty maintenance services and retail merchandise sales. Management highlighted cost reduction efforts and localized procurement as key to vehicle affordability, with specific mention of competitive pricing in China. Sales-type leasing revenue and associated costs became more significant as the leasing program scaled up through the year. Warranty expenses were consistently recorded as part of cost of revenues. Despite these detailed disclosures, management did not provide explicit forward guidance on COGS, focusing instead on qualitative commentary and per-vehicle cost metrics.

Forecasting credibility: None
No guidance versus actual pairs were provided for cost of goods sold, making it impossible to assess management's forecasting credibility for this topic. The available information consists solely of actual reported figures and qualitative commentary without forward-looking numerical guidance. Therefore, no credibility rating can be assigned based on the evidence.

Trends:
  - Increasing cost of automotive leasing revenue
  - Rising used vehicle cost of revenue
  - Growth in costs supporting non-warranty maintenance services
  - Expansion of sales-type leasing revenue and costs
  - Consistent recording of warranty expense within cost of revenues

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Cost reduction efforts and localized procurement
  - Impact of increased vehicle volumes on COGS
  - Introduction and scaling of sales-type leasing programs
  - Warranty expenses as a component of COGS

Key concerns:
  - No forward guidance on cost of goods sold limits ability to evaluate forecasting accuracy or management's predictive capability for this key metric.
  - The shift in cost recognition related to sales-type leasing programs introduces complexity in comparing period-over-period COGS.
  - No accounting or reporting inconsistencies were noted, but the evolving nature of leasing revenue and associated costs requires careful interpretation.

Conclusion:
Cost of goods sold increased substantially throughout 2021, driven by higher volumes in automotive leasing, used vehicle sales, and service-related activities. Management emphasized cost reduction initiatives and localized manufacturing to maintain vehicle affordability, particularly in China. The introduction and growth of sales-type leasing programs added new components to COGS, reflected in increasing sales-type leasing costs reported each period. Warranty expenses consistently formed part of COGS, underscoring ongoing service-related cost pressures. However, the absence of forward guidance on COGS prevents an assessment of management's forecasting reliability for this metric, leaving investors reliant on actual reported figures and qualitative explanations.
