[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: gross margin

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the course of 2021, gross margin trends showed a clear improvement in the automotive segment, with management expressing optimism about margin strength despite some headwinds, particularly in the energy generation and storage segment. Automotive gross margins steadily increased from around 26% in Q1 to a peak of 29.2% excluding credits in Q4, supported by cost reductions, factory utilization, and product mix improvements. Energy generation and storage margins remained negative or low throughout the year, impacted by product ramp challenges and manufacturing underutilization. Management's guidance generally anticipated margin improvements, especially in automotive, which were realized in the actual results reported in filings.

Forecasting credibility: High
There is one explicit guidance/actual pair with a verdict in Q4 2021 where management forecasted an increase in automotive gross margin, and the actual result met this expectation with a reported 29.2% margin excluding credits, the highest yet. Across the periods, management's optimistic commentary on automotive gross margin improvements was consistently supported by actual filing data showing steady margin increases. This pattern of met or exceeded expectations in automotive gross margin guidance supports a high credibility rating for management's forecasting in this area.

Trends:
  - Automotive gross margin steadily increasing
  - Energy generation and storage gross margin declining or negative
  - Improvement in services and other segment margins
  - Cost reduction and factory utilization driving margin gains

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Negative gross margins in energy storage and Solar Roof
  - Temporary manufacturing underutilization during product ramp
  - Impact of product mix on overall gross margin
  - Uncertainty in near-term margin impacts due to mixed headwinds and tailwinds

Key concerns:
  - Energy generation and storage segment consistently shows negative or declining gross margins, indicating ongoing challenges in this area.
  - Temporary manufacturing underutilization and product ramp issues in Solar Roof and Megapack affect margin stability.
  - No accounting or reporting inconsistencies were noted across the filings; actuals from filings provide reliable data.

Conclusion:
Gross margin performance in 2021 was characterized by strong and consistent improvement in the automotive segment, with margins rising from approximately 26% to over 29% by year-end, driven by cost efficiencies and production scale. Conversely, the energy generation and storage segment struggled with negative margins due to product ramp challenges and underutilization. Management's guidance on automotive gross margin increases was accurate and credible, as confirmed by filing-sourced actuals. Overall, the company's gross margin strength is primarily anchored in automotive operations, while energy-related segments remain a key area of concern.

[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: cost of goods sold

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the 2021 reporting periods, cost of goods sold (COGS) showed consistent increases primarily driven by higher volumes and values in used vehicle trade-ins, increased non-warranty maintenance services, and retail merchandise sales. Management highlighted ongoing cost reduction efforts and localized procurement to maintain vehicle affordability, particularly in China. Warranty expenses were consistently recorded as part of cost of revenues. Leasing-related cost of revenue also increased significantly as the leasing offering scaled. Despite these cost increases, management noted a reduction in cost of goods sold per vehicle year-over-year in Q2 2021, indicating some operational efficiency gains.

Forecasting credibility: None
No forward guidance on cost of goods sold was provided by management in any period, and thus no guidance-to-actual comparisons exist to assess forecasting credibility. All commentary on COGS was retrospective or descriptive rather than predictive. Therefore, it is not possible to rate management's forecasting credibility for this topic based on the available evidence.

Trends:
  - Increasing cost of automotive leasing revenue
  - Rising cost of services and other revenue
  - Growth in used vehicle cost of revenue
  - Ongoing cost reduction efforts
  - Stable inclusion of warranty expense in cost of revenues

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Impact of increased trade-in volumes and values
  - Costs associated with non-warranty maintenance services
  - Effect of retail merchandise sales growth
  - Localized procurement and manufacturing to control costs
  - Warranty expense as a consistent cost component

Key concerns:
  - No forward-looking guidance on cost of goods sold limits ability to evaluate management's forecasting accuracy or reliability for this key expense line.
  - The significant increases in cost of services and other revenue and automotive leasing costs highlight exposure to volume-driven cost fluctuations.
  - No accounting or reporting inconsistencies were noted across filings; warranty expense treatment remained consistent.

Conclusion:
Cost of goods sold increased substantially throughout 2021, driven by higher volumes and values in used vehicle trade-ins, expanded non-warranty maintenance services, and retail merchandise sales. Management emphasized cost reduction initiatives and localized procurement to maintain vehicle affordability, which contributed to a reported $5,000 year-over-year reduction in cost of goods sold per vehicle in Q2 2021. Warranty expenses consistently formed part of cost of revenues, and leasing-related costs rose as the leasing business scaled. However, the absence of forward guidance on cost of goods sold means management's ability to forecast this expense cannot be assessed, leaving uncertainty about future cost control effectiveness.
