TOPIC: Gross profit
======================================================================

[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: Gross profit

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the course of 2021, gross profit and gross margin showed a consistent upward trend, particularly in the automotive segment. Management frequently highlighted improvements in automotive gross margins, excluding regulatory credits, reaching record highs by Q4 2021. Energy generation and storage gross margins remained negative or declined due to product mix shifts and ramp-up challenges. Despite the lack of explicit forward guidance on gross profit, management expressed optimism about margin strength and cost reduction efforts throughout the year.

Forecasting credibility: None
No guidance versus actual pairs were provided for gross profit, so it is not possible to assess management's forecasting credibility for this topic. All commentary is qualitative or retrospective, without specific forward-looking quantitative targets that can be compared to actual results.

Trends:
  - Increasing automotive gross margin
  - Negative or declining energy segment gross margin
  - Improvement in overall gross margin year-over-year
  - Record automotive gross margins by Q4 2021

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Energy segment margin pressure due to product mix and ramp-up
  - Impact of regulatory credits on reported automotive margins
  - Cost reduction and factory utilization as margin drivers

Key concerns:
  - Energy generation and storage segment gross margin declined notably in 2021 due to higher Solar Roof proportion and Powerpack phase-out, indicating margin pressure in that business line.
  - No explicit forward guidance on gross profit or gross margin was given, limiting the ability to evaluate forecasting accuracy.
  - The mix of hardware and software in automotive gross margin was discussed as a future margin driver but remains uncertain.

Conclusion:
Gross profit, particularly in the automotive segment, improved steadily throughout 2021, with automotive gross margin increasing from approximately 25-26% in early 2021 to nearly 29-30% by year-end. This improvement was driven by cost reductions, increased production efficiency, and favorable product mix, including the ramp of Shanghai Model Y. Conversely, the energy generation and storage segment experienced negative gross margins due to product mix shifts and manufacturing underutilization during new product ramps. Management's commentary was optimistic about margin strength but did not provide explicit forward guidance on gross profit, preventing an assessment of forecasting credibility. Overall, the evidence shows a clear positive trajectory in automotive gross profit and margin, offset by challenges in the energy segment.

[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: selling, general and administrative

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the 2021 reporting periods, selling, general and administrative (SG&A) expenses showed significant increases, notably a 68% rise in Q1 2021 compared to the prior year. Stock-based compensation, particularly related to the 2018 CEO Performance Award, consistently contributed a substantial non-cash portion of SG&A expenses each quarter. Cash flow statements repeatedly highlighted that cash investments in SG&A were a key factor affecting operating cash flows. Additionally, a $43 million payment related to the SolarCity acquisition was recognized as a reduction in SG&A expenses in mid-2020 and referenced in subsequent filings. Marketing and advertising costs were consistently expensed as incurred within SG&A. No forward guidance on SG&A was provided by management throughout the periods reviewed.

Forecasting credibility: None
There are zero guidance versus actual pairs available for selling, general and administrative expenses, indicating that management does not provide forward guidance on this line item. As a result, it is not possible to assess forecasting credibility for SG&A based on the evidence provided. Any commentary or trends observed are descriptive and do not reflect forecasting accuracy.

Trends:
  - Significant increase in SG&A expenses
  - Consistent stock-based compensation expense within SG&A
  - Cash investments impacting operating cash flows
  - Recognition of SolarCity acquisition payment reducing SG&A
  - Marketing and advertising expensed as incurred

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Non-cash stock-based compensation as a major SG&A component
  - Cash flow sensitivity to SG&A spending
  - Accounting treatment of acquisition-related payments
  - Volatility in SG&A due to growth investments

Key concerns:
  - No forward guidance on SG&A limits ability to evaluate management's forecasting reliability for this expense category.
  - The large and variable stock-based compensation expense within SG&A could obscure underlying operational expense trends.
  - Recognition of acquisition-related payments as reductions to SG&A may affect comparability across periods.

Conclusion:
Selling, general and administrative expenses increased substantially during 2021, driven in part by stock-based compensation and growth-related cash investments. The consistent inclusion of stock-based compensation as a non-cash SG&A expense and the accounting for acquisition-related payments as reductions to SG&A are important factors in understanding the expense profile. Management does not provide forward guidance on SG&A, so forecasting credibility cannot be assessed. Overall, SG&A reflects both operational scaling and non-cash accounting elements, making it a complex but critical expense category to monitor.

[CORE RESEARCH] TSLA: Current liabilities

Quarters searched: 4
Filing sections searched: 77

Over the reported periods in 2021, total current liabilities showed a general increasing trend, rising from $14,877 million in Q1 to $18,051 million in Q3, before a slight decrease noted in Q4 filings. The increases were primarily driven by ramp-ups in production at Gigafactory Shanghai and the Fremont Factory, which led to higher accounts payable and accrued liabilities. Additionally, deferred revenue increased due to higher vehicle deliveries, and customer deposits were noted as part of current liabilities. Interest rate swaps were consistently recorded at fair value within other non-current assets or long-term liabilities, with changes recognized in other income or expense. The disclosures also highlighted the inclusion of customer deposits and the impact of operating lease vehicles introduced with Model Y direct leasing.

Forecasting credibility: None
No guidance versus actual pairs were provided for current liabilities, which is common for mechanical accounting line items. Therefore, it is not possible to assess management's forecasting credibility on this topic based on the available data.

Trends:
  - Increasing total current liabilities through Q3 2021
  - Higher accounts payable and accrued liabilities
  - Rising deferred revenue from vehicle deliveries
  - Inclusion of customer deposits in current liabilities

Recurring themes/risks:
  - Production ramp-up impacts on liabilities
  - Deferred revenue growth
  - Accounting for operating lease vehicles
  - Fair value measurement of interest rate swaps

Key concerns:
  - No forward guidance or forecast data on current liabilities limits assessment of management's predictive accuracy.
  - Consistent accounting treatment of interest rate swaps and operating lease vehicles noted, with no apparent inconsistencies.
  - Partial filing excerpts for Q4 2021 liabilities are truncated, limiting full visibility into year-end current liabilities composition.

Conclusion:
Current liabilities increased notably during 2021, driven by operational factors such as production ramp-up and higher vehicle deliveries, which increased accounts payable, accrued liabilities, and deferred revenue. Customer deposits are also a component of current liabilities, reflecting ongoing business activities. The accounting treatment for related items like interest rate swaps and operating lease vehicles appears consistent across periods. However, the absence of guidance and forecast comparisons means management's ability to predict current liabilities cannot be evaluated. Overall, current liabilities reflect operational growth and are reported with standard accounting practices without evident red flags.
